The 2026 State of
Retail-First EV Charging.

This report aims to uncover the relationship between EV fast charging station site performance and nearby amenities, and provide insights into the evolution of EV charging infrastructure.

About this report

We wanted to know which EV fast charging stations perform best based on their retail amenities.

In partnership with Paren, we looked at the most complete dataset of charging station performance and amenities, comprising over 4,000 DCFC stations across America.

Methodology
1
DC fast charging stations rated 100 kW per port or higher. Stations below 100 kW are predominantly Level 2 destination stations, such as those at hotels or workplaces, serving a different use case from retail fast charging.
2
Rich session data only. The 4,105 stations in our dataset include Electrify America, EVgo, ChargePoint, Ford, IONNA, Applegreen, Red-E, Shell Recharge, FLO, Blink, and Electric Era. Tesla does not provide Supercharger session data.

We pivoted the data across three different views:

View 01

Any amenity within 0.25 miles

A wide net: everything within a 5-minute walk. Good for identifying corridor effects and broad retail context.

View 02

Any amenity within 0.1 miles

A tight net: physically adjacent to the station. Immediate parking lot, neighboring storefronts. Most of our deep analysis relies on this view.

View 03

The single closest amenity

A minimalist view that isolates just the immediate amenity. Good for cutting through density effects to identify which anchor matters most.

Definitions

Three terms that show up throughout this report.

We use these concepts repeatedly. Here's what they mean and how we calculated them.

01
Key term
Amenity
An amenity is any nearby business or service drivers might use during a charging session. We mapped every business near a station to one of 17 types, Shopping Mall, Pharmacy, Grocery, Coffee, Sit-Down Restaurant, Big Box, Fashion Retail, and so on. Amenities are based on Google Places business-type tags, cleaned and consolidated.
02
Key term
100 kW+ fast charging station
A 100 kW+ fast charging station is a high-power DC fast charging station that can charge a typical EV in 20–40 minutes. This report includes stations rated 100 kW or higher. Slower stations serve a different customer, destination charging overnight or at workplaces.
03
Key term
Sessions per day
Our primary measure of utilization. SPD is the total number of charging sessions a site completes in a typical day, averaged across available history. Across the 4,105 stations in this dataset, the overall median is 9 SPD. Any amenity above 9 outperforms the average.

Finding 01
Density compounds. Drivers want options.

A single anchor, like a big box store, performs well on its own. But stations with 11 or more nearby amenities see roughly 7× the utilization of isolated ones.

Finding 02
Grocery is the runaway winner.

Fast stations near a grocery store see a median of 42 sessions per day, the highest of any amenity type in the dataset. Grocery stores combine high traffic, repeat visits, and dwell times that match a typical fast charge.

Finding 03
Most stations aren't near the highest-performing amenity types.
6.7%

Only 6.7% of stations sit near a grocery store, the top-performing amenity. Mall penetration is 3.7%. Pharmacy is 14.5%. These are the amenity types that drive the most utilization, and they're the least represented in today's fast charging network.

Station health matters. A lot.
6×

A reliable station sees 6× the utilization of an unreliable one, regardless of what's near it. An unreliable station is invisible to drivers. They get removed from routing apps and rarely re-tried.

The amenity landscape

Which amenities are actually near America's fast charging stations?

Before we look at performance, here's the mix we're working with. We mapped every business within 0.1 miles of every 100 kW+ station to one of 17 amenity types. Some show up almost everywhere, while others are much rarer.

Quick Serve, Sit-Down, and Coffee show up next to roughly half of all 200 kW+ stations. These are the ubiquitous amenities. Big Box, Bank, Fashion Retail, and Grocery sit in the middle, present near roughly a quarter of stations. Pharmacy, Shopping Mall, and Health are the under-penetrated tier, near 14–21% of stations each, despite performing well when they're present (more on that in Finding 03).

Density

More amenities, more sessions.

A great anchor like Walmart or Target performs well on its own. But the biggest performance lever in our entire dataset is amenity density: how many distinct amenities sit within a 2-minute walk of the station.

Charging stations with amenities nearby see a median of 9 sessions per day. Charging stations with no amenities nearby see a median of 3. That's nearly 3.5× more utilization when you put a fast charging station near retail.

Station health

A reliable station is visible. An unreliable one isn't.

Location is the setup. Reliability is the payoff. A reliable station at a mediocre location can outperform an unreliable station at an exceptional one.

A fast charging station's health score strongly predicts its utilization, and not by a little.

That's a 6× gap driven entirely by reliability.

The hardware and the operator matter as much as the location. A cheaper station with worse uptime doesn't just frustrate drivers, it changes customer habits. Broken stations get demoted or removed from routing apps and are never re-tried.

Malls are the highest-density winner.

Only 3.7% of stations are near a shopping mall, the lowest penetration of any top-performing amenity type.

What makes a good charging station

Here's what makes the best charging stations: reliable hardware in a retail-rich location.

After analyzing 4,105 fast charging stations, two factors predict utilization more than anything else. The hardware works. And it's surrounded by the right things.

6×
Healthy hardware

Health scores above 90 see 6× the utilization of stations below 90. An unreliable station is invisible to drivers.

5×
Retail context

Charging stations with at least one amenity within 0.1 miles see nearly 3.5× the median utilization (9 SPD) of charging stations with zero amenities nearby (3 SPD). Even one nearby business gives drivers somewhere to be while their car charges.

10×
Density compounds

Stations with 11 or more amenities within 0.1 miles see 7× the utilization of stations with no nearby amenities. More options means more visits.

3.2×
The right anchors

All amenities have a positive impact on utilization, but some have a stronger uplift than others. Differentiators (malls, pharmacies, grocery) drive the biggest gains in SPD, while baseline tenants (coffee, QSR, sit-down) help build the surrounding context that makes a site work.

What this means for retailers

For most of retail history, the parking lot was overhead. That's changing.

The right fast charging station in the right parking lot is now a traffic driver, a dwell-time extender, and a predictable source of return visits. The question isn't whether to add fast charging. It's whether you'll add it on your terms, or react to a competitor who moved first.

The findings in this report aren't proprietary to Electric Era. They're true of fast charging in American retail, whoever installs it. But if you're evaluating partners, the data points to three things that matter more than most retailers realize:

Hardware built for retail dwell times

Underpowered stations force drivers to extend their visit beyond what they planned. For a 20–40 minute grocery run or pharmacy visit, delivering up to 400 kW per port matches driver behavior. Electric Era's RE400 stations are built around that window.

Uptime as a contractual guarantee, not a marketing phrase

The data shows that reliability is worth 6× utilization. Automated monitoring, remediation, and OTA updates allow providers like Electric Era to catch failures before they frustrate drivers.

A driver experience that protects your brand

When a station fails, the driver automatically thinks less of the retailer whose parking lot they're in. Electric Era's combination of hardware reliability, remote diagnostics, and real-time driver support means that when something goes wrong, drivers are able to successfully charge without giving up on your store.

Owning vs. hosting

Who owns the station changes the business outcome.

The same station in the same parking lot can produce very different results for a retailer depending on whether they own it or host it.

CPO-owned model
A charging provider owns and operates the station

In the CPO-owned model, a charging network installs and operates the equipment. The retailer receives a modest site host fee or simply benefits from foot traffic. The CPO controls the pricing, the driver experience, the branding, and the data. For the retailer, the station is essentially a utility in their parking lot, not a business asset.

Predictable, low-effort income
No capital outlay
CPO brand, not yours, at the point of experience
No access to session data or customer insights
Limited control over pricing, uptime, or experience
Retailer-owned model
The retailer owns the station and controls the experience

When a retailer owns the infrastructure, the station becomes part of the store experience. Pricing, loyalty integration, dwell-time promotions, and the driver relationship all stay with the retailer. Session data flows into their own analytics. A failed charge reflects on the retailer's brand regardless of who owns the hardware, but the recovery and the relationship belong to whoever controls the station.

Full control over driver experience and branding
Session data and customer behavior insights
Loyalty program and promotional integration
Revenue upside scales with utilization
Requires capital investment and operational partner

The data in this report describes utilization outcomes, not ownership structure. But the retailers who will capture the most value from high-performing amenity locations are the ones who treat charging as an owned asset, not an outsourced utility. The difference between a grocery store that hosts a station and one that owns a station is the difference between a line item and a competitive advantage.
